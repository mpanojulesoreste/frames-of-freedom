Final Assessment

Choose between a creative project or comparative analysis essay to demonstrate your understanding of freedom and resistance in the Caribbean.

Overview

The final assessment offers two options for demonstrating your understanding of the themes, histories, and artistic expressions of resistance explored throughout this course. You may choose either a creative project or a comparative analysis essay.

Important Requirement

Students must meet with course instructor(s) via office hours before the submission of the final assignment. Please cite all sources in Chicago style and include a bibliography.

Assessment Options

Option 1

Creative Project

Using primary and secondary sources (from this course and/or outside materials), students will individually produce a creative project in a medium of their choice—such as a visual artwork, short film, photo essay, original music, or literary work.

This project should illuminate your personal vision of what freedom can look like in the Caribbean, grounded in the histories, resistance movements, and artistic practices explored in the course. Your work should demonstrate an understanding of how Caribbean artists have challenged colonial legacies and reimagined liberation through creative expression.

Include a 1–2 page artist's statement explaining your project's connection to course themes, historical contexts, and chosen sources.

All sources must be cited in Chicago style, and a bibliography must be included.

Suggested Media

Visual Art
Short Film
Photo Essay
Original Music
Literary Work

Option 2

Comparative Analysis Essay

Using primary sources and secondary sources from any week of the course, write a double-spaced comparative analysis essay (5–7 pages) that explores contested visions of freedom in the Caribbean.

Your essay should make an original argument about how different artists, thinkers, or works have framed freedom in light of historical, political, and cultural contexts. Consider how various media—music, photography, literature, or film—express resistance, shape political consciousness, and respond to ongoing struggles against colonialism, globalization, or displacement.

Questions to Consider

  • How do the authors/artists conceptualize freedom, and how do their visions differ or converge?
  • What role does tone, form, or word choice play in shaping their political message?
  • How are their visions informed by the specific historical moment in which they write or create?

Students must cite all sources in Chicago style and include a bibliography.
