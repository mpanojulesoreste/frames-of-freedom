Literary Work

This week examines literature as a critical site of resistance in the Caribbean. Focusing on poetry, drama, and prose, we will explore how writers respond to histories of colonization, displacement, and power structures by crafting narratives that reclaim voice, identity, and agency. Literature in the Caribbean often bridges oral traditions, political critique, and experimental aesthetics, functioning both as a historical witness and a visionary tool for imagining alternative futures. We will analyze how Caribbean authors use language, genre, and form to interrogate imperialism, challenge systems of domination, resist cultural erasure, articulate visions of justice, liberation, and imagine alternative Caribbean futures.

Objectives

  1. Analyze how Caribbean literary texts resist colonialism, imperialism, and cultural erasure.
  2. Explore the relationship between form, voice, and literary techniques in Caribbean writing.
  3. Examine how literature constructs memory, identity, and collective resistance.
  4. Compare how different genres (poetry, play, essay) articulate resistance, critique power, and engage audiences.

Discussion Questions

  1. What rhetorical or literary strategies (symbolism, voice, irony, structure) do the authors use to express collective resistance and reimagine historical narratives?
  2. In what ways do genre and form amplify or constrain the message in each text?

In-Class Activity

    Students will work in groups to perform a close reading of an assigned passage (from poetry, drama, or play). Each group will identify literary devices and analyze how these strategies express resistance, cultural identity, or critique of power. Presentations will link form to message, reinforcing literature’s resistive and imaginative power.

Assigned materials

Primary Source

This is the Dark Time, My Love

Martin Carter

This poem describes the oppression, sorrow, and fear Guyana faces during the US intervention, highlighting themes of colonialism, oppression and cultural exploitation. It utilizes different literary devices and techniques like metaphor, personification and juxtaposition. It highlights the pervasive American cultural and political influence on the West Indies and serves as a significant piece of social commentary on power dynamics and marginalization.

Read Full Poem
Carter, Martin. "This is the dark time, my love" In World of Poetry. Third Edition, edited by Mark McWatt and Hazel Simmonds- McDonald, 123. London: Hodder Education, 2015.
Primary Source

Ti Jean and His Brothers

Derek Walcott

This play is a West Indian folktale where three brothers confront the Devil. The play delves into themes of colonialism, capitalism, and the essence of humanity, with the youngest brother, Ti-Jean, ultimately triumphing through his wit and defiance.

Access Play
Walcott, Derek. Ti-Jean and His Brothers. [1st electronic ed.]. Alexandria, VA: Alexander Street Press, 2003.
Primary Source

West Indies, U.S.A.

Stewart Brown

This poem delves into the intricate and often fraught relationship between Puerto and the United States, focusing on the experiences of Puerto Rico encountering the U.S. Through vivid imagery and sharp contrasts, the poem critiques the idealized American dream versus the often harsh realities of racism, discrimination, and socio-economic inequality faced by Caribbean people. It highlights the pervasive American cultural and political influence on the West Indies and serves as a significant piece of social commentary on power dynamics and marginalization.

Read Full Poem
Brown, Stewart. "West Indies, U.S.A." In World of Poetry. Third Edition, edited by Mark McWatt and Hazel Simmonds- McDonald, 42. London: Hodder Education, 2015.
Primary Source

A Small Place

Jamaica Kincaid

This book offers a scathing critique of an Antiguan native, intertwining personal memories with a sharp analysis of its socio-political landscape. The book highlights the enduring negative impacts of British colonialism, a corrupt and ineffective government prioritizing tourism over its citizens, and the detrimental effects of the tourist industry itself, which she argues exacerbates poverty and inequality. Ultimately, the book presents an unflinching examination of how Antigua's problems are deeply rooted in its colonial past and the ongoing exploitation of its people and resources.

Read Full Book
Kincaid, Jamaica. A Small Place. 1st ed. New York: Farrar, Straus, Giroux, 1988
Secondary Source

Practices of Resistance in the Caribbean: Narratives, Aesthetics and Politics

Wiebke Beushausen, Miriam Brandel, Joseph Farquharson, Marius Littschwager, Annika McPherson, and Julia Roth, eds.

This source concludes how authors, driven by different ideologies, intermingle Creole and French in their works, enacting "translanguage marooning." Writers also explore the freedom and flexibility of linguistic contact, symbolizing a deeper exploration of language.

Access Book
Beushausen, Wiebke, Miriam Brandel, Joseph Farquharson, Marius Littschwager, Annika McPherson, and Julia Roth, eds. Practices of resistance in the Caribbean: Narratives, aesthetics and politics. Routledge, 2018.
Secondary Source

Resistance Poems: The Voice of Martin Carter.

Edward Kamau Brathwaite

This source introduces Martin Carter’s and other Caribbean work as works that stems from a general political anticipation of anti-colonial success rather than personal circumstances. The source expresses challenges of the West Indies as former colonial territories that still struggle to achieve political independence and forge a proud national identity based on love and a positive perception.

Access Article
Brathwaite, Edward Kamau. "Resistance Poems: The Voice of Martin Carter." Caribbean Quarterly 23, no. 2-3 (1977): 7-23.
Secondary Source

From the Traumas of the Caribbean to a Revival of Resistant Literature: A West Indian Discourse.

Nayera Mohammed Hassan

This source examines the historical traumas and migrations of Caribbean peoples, focusing on their efforts to forge a collective cultural identity amidst displacement. It highlights the rise of a resistant Caribbean literature that voices their anxieties and confusions stemming from both geographical and psychological dislocation, particularly as migration to Europe became common in the post-colonial era.

Access Article
Hassan, Nayera Mohammed. “From the Traumas of the Caribbean to a Revival of Resistant Literature: A West Indian Discourse.” Journal of Ethnic and Cultural Studies 7, no. 2 (2020): 173–94.
Secondary Source

Caribbean literary discourse: Voice and cultural identity in the anglophone Caribbean.

Barbara Lalla, Jean D'Costa, and Velma Pollard.

This source examines the historical traumas and migrations of Caribbean peoples, focusing on their efforts to forge a collective cultural identity amidst displacement. It highlights the rise of a resistant Caribbean literature that voices their anxieties and confusions stemming from both geographical and psychological dislocation, particularly as migration to Europe became common in the post-colonial era.

Access Book
Lalla, Barbara, Jean D'Costa, and Velma Pollard. Caribbean literary discourse: Voice and cultural identity in the anglophone Caribbean. University of Alabama Press, 2014.
Secondary Source

Resistances in Caribbean Literature (1930s to the present)

Yolanda Martínez-San Miguel

This source explores Caribbean literature of resistance by comparing literary production across the Spanish, French, and Anglo Caribbean from the 1930s to the present. It focuses on texts that address racial and ethnic debates in defining Caribbean identity, in relation to colonial, postcolonial, and neocolonial conceptualizations of the region and its geopolitical imaginary.

Access Book
Martínez-San Miguel, Yolanda. "Resistances in Caribbean Literature (1930s to the present)." In The Routledge History of Latin American Culture, pp. 94-114. Routledge, 2017.

 

Weekly Writing Assignment

    Write a 1-2 page, double-spaced discussion post responding to the following: Analyze how one literary work from this week expresses collective resistance and reimagine historical narratives? How does the author use structure, voice, or genre to assert identity or critique systems of power? Additionally, briefly mention how a secondary source expands, contextualizes n your understanding of the literary work.
