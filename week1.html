Sonic Revolutions: Music as Resistance in the Caribbean

This week introduces the course by centering music—specifically reggae and calypso—as foundational modes of resistance and cultural survival in the Caribbean from the late twentieth century through the twenty-first century. We will explore how Caribbean musicians have used music not only as a form of artistic expression but also as a powerful political tool to resist colonial legacies, racial hierarchies, state violence, and economic exploitation. These musical genres helped build collective consciousness, preserve cultural memory, and articulate radical visions of liberation and justice. Through close listening and lyrical analysis, we will consider how music reflects, critiques, and reimagines the Caribbean experience in the face of historical and ongoing oppression.

Objectives

  1. Understand reggae and calypso as forms of creative resistance grounded in anti-colonial, anti-capitalist, and Afro-diasporic traditions.
  2. Examine how reggae and calypso functions as both historical archive and social intervention
  3. Understand the role of music in raising consciousness and mobilizing communities.
  4. Interpret the use of metaphor, rhythm, narrative, and performance in articulating cultural identity, protest, and solidarity.
  5. Compare how various artists employed musical form and performance to challenge dominant historical and build cultural dialogues.

Discussion Questions

  1. How do reggae and calypso artists mobilize music to challenge colonialism, racism, and social, economical and political injustice?
  2. What role does music play in shaping political awareness, preserving memory, and sustaining resistance movements in Caribbean communities?

In-Class Activity

    In small groups, students will analyze selected lyrics and performances, identifying key rhetorical and sonic strategies used to convey resistance, cultural memory, and political critique. Presentations will emphasize how sound, metaphor, repetition, and cultural references create layered meanings within the songs.

Assigned Materials

Primary Source - Reggae

Can’t Breathe

Kabaka Pyramid

This song expresses deep frustration with systemic oppression, poverty, and social injustice, especially affecting youth who are denied education and opportunity. The singer describes a suffocating environment marked by economic stagnation, toxic food systems, and societal neglect. Despite the suffering, there’s a resilient call for change through consciousness, natural living, and revolution of the mind.

Watch on Youtube
Kabaka Pyramid - Can’t Breathe (Official Video), 2017.
Primary Source - Reggae

Crazy Baldhead

Bob Marley

This song calls for the removal of oppressive figures who have exploited and disrespected the people who originally built and cultivated the land. It reflects anger at historical and ongoing injustices, including slavery, land theft, and corrupt systems like biased education and religion. Ultimately, it’s a declaration of resistance and reclaiming dignity and space.

Watch on Youtube
Marley, Bob, and The Wailers. "Crazy Baldhead." Rastaman Vibration, Island Records, 1976.
Primary Source - Reggae

Buffalo Soldier

Bob Marley

This song draws a connection between these soldiers and the Rastafarian movement, emphasizing a legacy of resistance and pride. It encourages historical awareness as a foundation for self-understanding and empowerment. The “Buffalo Soldier” symbolizes resilience, survival, and the struggle for identity amid displacement and exploitation.

Watch on Youtube
Bob Marley- Buffalo Soldier, 2008.
Primary Source - Reggae

Whiteman Country

Mutabaruka

This song critiques colonial and post-colonial power dynamics, emphasizing how Black individuals are marginalized and pressured to assimilate. With irony and cultural references, it highlights the identity loss and systemic inequality faced.

Watch on Youtube
Mutabaruka - Whiteman Country, 1992.
Primary Source - Calypso

Qualifications Of A Politician

Mighty Chalkdust

This song satirically exposes the hypocrisy and corruption in politics, portraying politicians as dishonest, self-serving, and manipulative. Through a father explaining to his son what it takes to become a politician. It mocks the idea that no formal education is needed—only a mastery of lies, betrayal, and selfish ambition. Ultimately, it presents politics as a game of deceit where public service is sacrificed for personal gain.

Watch on Youtube
Mighty Chalkdust - Qualifications Of A Politician, 2011.
Primary Source - Calypso

Capitalism Gone Mad

Mighty Sparrow

This song portrays a society where only the wealthy or corrupt can thrive, while working people struggle under inflated prices, stagnant wages, and systemic inequality. The lyrics satirize capitalism run amok—where basic goods, services, and even death have become unaffordable luxuries. Ultimately, it expresses frustration with a system that exploits the poor while enriching the elite.

Watch on Youtube
Mighty Sparrow - Capitalism Gone Mad, 1982.
Primary Source - Calypso

Without Love

King Short Shirt

This song reflects on humanity’s failure to evolve morally despite the passage of centuries, emphasizing how the ongoing pursuit of power and fame leads to tragedy and social decay. He highlights the widespread injustice, inequality, and corruption that persist in societies lacking love and compassion. Ultimately, it is a call for moral and emotional renewal as the only path to true societal progress.

Watch on Youtube
King Short Shirt - Without Love, 2015.
Primary Source - Calypso

Illusion

King Short Shirt

This song powerfully critiques the illusion of freedom in the Caribbean, arguing that despite the end of formal slavery, true liberation has yet to be achieved. It emphasizes that political independence is meaningless without economic and social control, and calls for unity across the region to fight continued oppression.

Watch on Youtube
King Short Shirt - Illusion, 2013.
Secondary Source

Resistance and Subversion in Caribbean Popular Music

Mike Alleyne

This source examines how anti-establishment themes in popular music, particularly Caribbean styles, are often diluted or co-opted by the music industry. It explores the political implications of this process, the decline of incisive critique, and how the profit-driven nature of the industry can ironically facilitate the distribution of oppositional culture, even as it reshapes it. The piece focuses on Bob Marley's career and his relationship with Island Records as a case study to understand these dynamics and the broader commodification of dissent in music.

Access Text
Alleyne, Mike. "Resistance and Subversion in Caribbean Popular Music." Griot 16, no. 1 (Spring, 1997): 58.
Secondary Source

Calypso Poetics

Kela Nnarka Francis

This source proposes developing a calypso poetics—a hermeneutic approach involving a five-point strategy to analyze elements like platform, genre, rhetorical devices, moods/rhythms, and persona. This system aims to move beyond mere likes and dislikes towards a more objective, academic understanding of calypso.

Access Text
Francis, Kela Nnarka. "Calypso Poetics." A Companion to African Rhetoric (2022): 227.
Secondary Source

We kind of music

Renu Juneja

This source explores calypso as a unique form of political music, originating from Trinidad's. While responding to current issues, calypso as a genre embodies a long-standing tradition reflecting the history, culture, and concerns of its people, differentiating it from crisis-oriented protest music in the West.

Access Text
Juneja, Renu. "We kind of music." Popular Music & Society13, no. 1 (1989): 37-51.
Secondary Source

Themes of war, politics and health education in Calypso music.

Agatha Lowe

This excerpt discusses music's role as a pervasive communicative force within mass media, capable of influencing moods, attitudes, and behaviors, and serving functions like education, rebellion, and socialization. It notes that Caribbean calypsonians music is aimed at educating and entertaining.

Weekly Writing Assignment

    Write a 1-2 page, double-spaced discussion post responding to the following: How do the musical texts assigned this week document and resist structures of political oppression in Caribbean societies? Choose one reggae and one calypso song. Analyze how their lyrics, rhythms, and performance styles function as political tools. Then, connect your analysis to at least one secondary source—how does it deepen or complicate your understanding of the primary work?
