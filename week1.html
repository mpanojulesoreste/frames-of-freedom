Week 1 – Sonic Revolutions: Music as Resistance in the Caribbean

This week introduces the course by centering music—specifically reggae and calypso—as foundational modes of resistance and cultural survival in the Caribbean from the late twentieth century through the twenty-first century. We will explore how Caribbean musicians have used music not only as a form of artistic expression but also as a powerful political tool to resist colonial legacies, racial hierarchies, state violence, and economic exploitation. These musical genres helped build collective consciousness, preserve cultural memory, and articulate radical visions of liberation and justice. Through close listening and lyrical analysis, we will consider how music reflects, critiques, and reimagines the Caribbean experience in the face of historical and ongoing oppression.

Objectives

  1. Understand reggae and calypso as forms of creative resistance grounded in anti-colonial, anti-capitalist, and Afro-diasporic traditions.
  2. Examine how reggae and calypso functions as both historical archive and social intervention
  3. Understand the role of music in raising consciousness and mobilizing communities.
  4. Interpret the use of metaphor, rhythm, narrative, and performance in articulating cultural identity, protest, and solidarity.
  5. Compare how various artists employed musical form and performance to challenge dominant historical and build cultural dialogues.

Discussion Questions

  1. How do reggae and calypso artists mobilize music to challenge colonialism, racism, and social, economical and political injustice?
  2. What role does music play in shaping political awareness, preserving memory, and sustaining resistance movements in Caribbean communities?

In- Class Activity

    In small groups, students will analyze selected lyrics and performances, identifying key rhetorical and sonic strategies used to convey resistance, cultural memory, and political critique. Presentations will emphasize how sound, metaphor, repetition, and cultural references create layered meanings within the songs.

Assigned Materials

Primary Source

Can’t Breathe

Kabaka Pyramid

This song expresses deep frustration with systemic oppression, poverty, and social injustice, especially affecting youth who are denied education and opportunity. The singer describes a suffocating environment marked by economic stagnation, toxic food systems, and societal neglect. Despite the suffering, there’s a resilient call for change through consciousness, natural living, and revolution of the mind.
